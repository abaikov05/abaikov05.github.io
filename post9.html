I’m not entirely sure I grasped every twist, but Florence Knapp’s book The Names feels like three novels in one. This piece of art thrills and sometimes overreaches.

In a beautyfuly writen prose, Knapp explores how his one choice — naming a child — splits the reality into three parallel lives for Cora and her son.

At the begining I found myself doubting whether the tripartite structure would hold my attention, yet each part pulls you back and forth with fresh and high emotional stakes.

The novel opens in October 1987, as Cora walks through storm debris to register her baby’s birth — a scene both sreal, sad and strangely epic.

As Cora weighs "Bear", "Julian", and "Gordon", you can just sense her fear, her hope and her trembling agency — and I’m left feeling if any name really matters.

Every seven years the timelines fork, showing how domestic abuse, resilience and love unfold differentl in each reality.

Characters cross over gently entangeling the three worlds together in a way that felt unexpectedly tender.

Some critics fault the one-dimensional villainy of Gordon, but I wasn’t entirely convinced he was meant to be more than a force of nature — still, it left me unsettled

Goodreads reports an average rating of 4.27/5 from nearly 3,000 readers, suggesting many share my mixed wonderment and harried admiration .

While it occasionally feels schematic, The Names ultimately asks more questions than it answers — and I can’t decide if that’s frustrating or exhilarating

In the end, this is less a book about names and more a meditation on choice, chance, and the fragile architecture of identity — a rare debut that lingers
